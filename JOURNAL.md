# 5/4/2026 2 AM - Created Battery, Audio System Schematics

_Time spent: 5.75h_

After implementing the all-important USB-C ports, it was time to focus my attention towards the lesser, but still hugely important parts of the AU_01. 

To start, I researched various different multi-cell li-ion battery chargers. Initially, I wanted to use a BQ25730 IC from TI for its very fast maximum charging speed, but found that its application schematic required four external MOSFETs, without any recommendation for what model or parameters to even look for. I started researching suitable MOSFETs for this chip, but eventually I found that the schematic would take up too much space on the board, so I abandoned the idea of using that IC.

![Screenshot 2026-05-04 at 12.06.23 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777860387811-e7rdvz.png)

I then started reasearching again, finding a bunch of interesting options from TI again. Most notably, I found a much more integrated IC, the BQ25798, that incorporated all its MOSFETs into its package, saving me both valuable board space and also the headache and uncertainty of selecting my own MOSFETs for the charger. This did come with a tradeoff of only allowing around 60W of maximum charging power into my 4S pack. For an 80Wh system, that means it would take a minimum of around 1h 20m to charge the battery from 0 to 100%. It isn't as fast as I hoped to get it, but again, this thing has more battery capacity than my laptop and sips around the same or less power.

![Screenshot 2026-05-04 at 12.07.08 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777860432225-9dy063.png)

So there was my battery charger choice confimed, so I implemented the application schematic for it on a separate sheet, and that's when I ran into a problem. The BQ25798 requires some configuration and real-time management through I2C, which is easy enough to implement in my SoM, but that's where the problem lies. If I were to link the I2C up to my SoM, it would require the full system to be on just for the battery to charge, which would be a huge oversight that would affect usability. So for this application, I opted to use a tiny MAX32660 MCU (the same model used in the MS_01 studs!) because of its ridiculous power efficiency, tiny size and minimal external component count. After all, I only need this thing to write a couple of I2C registers and manage basic state.

![Screenshot 2026-05-04 at 12.07.46 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777860471621-rhgy8v.png)

Next, it was time to move on to designing the audio system circuitry. I want the AU_01 to have an integrated DSP and three amplifiers, one for each speaker driver. For the DSP, I used the easiest-to-program and most integrated option available, which is the ADAU1701 by Analog Devices. This DSP chip integrates DACs and ADCs, as well as internal logic circuitry and can be programmed easily using the SigmaStudio GUI. 

![Screenshot 2026-05-04 at 12.09.55 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777860600937-f9meo1.png)

I chose the PAM8320 amplifier chip for the subwoofer, as it requires a minimal number of external components and is a well-known amplifier IC that can reliably deliver 20W for the Tectonic Elements driver. For the Dayton Audio stereo speaker, which only require 2W each, I used the PAM8302 amplifier chips for the same reasons as the larger amplifier, and I implemented the application schematics for all three chips. Conveniently, the PAM8302 ICs run perfectly on 5V, and the PAM8320 is fine with a 12V input. So the 5V supply for the smaller speakers can be shared with the SoM, and the 12V supply (after replacing the 1A regulator with a beefy 3A one) of the larger speaker can be shared with the OLED display, as its driver board takes a 12V input.

![Screenshot 2026-05-04 at 12.10.25 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777860629750-751lmg.png)

I then implemented the application schematic for the ADAU1701, and wired it so that it takes an I2S input and outputs three different analog signals, one for each
amplifier. 

![Screenshot 2026-05-04 at 12.10.59 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777860662107-1c1qes.png)

During this, I ran into a problem, where the ADAU1701 specified using a 12.288MHz oscillator to generate a clock signal on its MCLK pin. This pin is also used by I2S, where the SoM provides its own MCLK for the chip to function at. Since MCLK can be driven at multiple clock frequencies (based on the sampling rate of the audio being played), I thought that foregoing the external oscillator and using the SoM's clock was a good choice. I also chose to do this because any mismatch in the MCLK between the chip's oscillator and the SoM's clock would result in the signal from the SoM (which depends on ITS clock) being jumbled up.

However, I realised that the ADAU1701 would require an instant clock signal upon power-on, and the SoM would definitely have some delay in turning on its MCLK. Also, since I'm using Android, it automatically limits its sampling rate to 48kHz, which results in a perfect 12.288MHz clock everytime. Therefore, I kept the external oscillator and left the SoM's MCLK signal unconnected. 

Now, the schematic for the entire AU_01 was pretty much done, and the only thing I needed to do was to add some sensors and supporting circuitry for a USB webcam.

# 4/28/2026 1 AM - Created USB-C Schematics

_Time spent: 5h_

Sooooo, I had to start actually creating the schematic diagrams for the various functions I want the AU_01 to support, and the most important (and probebly the hardest) one that I want to tackle are the USB-C ports that make up the primary method of interfacing with the device, and also the primary charging method.

I decided that I will include two USB-C ports on the device: One for USB-PD charging and USB2.0 data transmission for basic data transfer. For this, I used the STUSB4531 from ST, as it is a modern PD controller capable of running completely autonomously and is powered by the VBUS, so it can even charge completely dead batteries. Furthermore, the PD controller integrates a gate driver for a MOSFET for switching VBUS on and off, while also being able to negotiate contracts of more than 100W.

![Screenshot 2026-04-28 at 11.13.08 am](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777338793806-78nxki.png)

The chip, however, only supports being a USB-PD sink, so it can only negotiate contracts where it takes power from an external source. This is why this port isn't good for unpowered peripherals (like keyboard/mouse dongles), as they simply won't receive any power. Additionally, the STUSB4531 hogs the CC pins on the USB-C port for itself, and it doesn't support USB-3.0 muxing across the port, so it will also be limited to USB-2.0 speeds. Considering the newest iPhones have that speed across their only USB-C port (yikes), I think the AU_01 having a full-speed USB-3.0 port in addition to the charging port is perfectly fine.

![Screenshot 2026-04-28 at 11.16.03 am](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777338970850-mjcg5y.png)

Following the application schematic for the STUSB4531, and ddding the same high-speed low-capacitance ESD protector chips as I used in the HDMI port to the USB-2.0 data lines, the electrical design for the charging USB-C port was done. It can negotiate PD contracts up to 100W at 20V, which is perfect to charge the AU_01's battery in a reasonable time. Of course, there is a little bit of programming that needs to be done to the PD controller IC, but that only involves changing a few values in its NVM using an STM32 Nucleo board. That's why I've added a pin header for the chip's programming as well.

Next up was the design of the full-speed USB-3.0 port that also needs to provide power as a source to any peripherals or small dongles. This needed a set of different chips, which includes the FUSB302 Type-C controller and the TPS2547 power controller. The FUSB302 handles the CC pins and determines the USB-C plug's orientation. This then sends a signal to the I-CORE3576Q38 via I2C to let it know which orientation the port is in, which then results in the system module multiplexing its USB-3.0 signal to connect to the device.

In normal USB-3.0 outputs from a module, the signal multiplexing would be handled externally on the carrier board, but curiously the module I'm using has a multiplexer already inside. I used the FUSB302 to make sure that Linux recognises the device and lets the USB signal be routed to the correct pins.

To make sure any peripherals have adequate power supplied by the AU_01, I used the TPS2547 from Texas Instruments, which monitors the VBUS and negotiates power contracts up to 15W not using the CC pins, but the USB-2.0 data lines in an older protocol called BC1.2.

I've always been intrigued by the form factor of basically putting a phone on your arm, but questioned its viability as a separate device because, well, you *can* just strap a phone to your arm and have an armband computer.

That's until I realised the amazing things that can be designed onto a device meant for your arm, such as a huge battery, huge curved screen, and the ability to actually fit off-the-shelf processors that can rival those in modern phones (All the *real* phone processors are tiny, but completely inaccessible to a random teen like me) plus, let's face it, it's pretty awesome to have a sci-fi computer on your arm.

So, with the goal of maximizing the potential benefits of putting a beefy phone on an arm, I started searching for the integral components this whole project will be built around. Most importantly, I wanted to get the display locked in.

I started on AliExpress, searching for OLED and AMOLED displays with touch functionality, and found an abundance of listings with either sky-high prices, or a lack of crucial features like the ability to bend (Flat displays don't mix well with round arms) or just being like 5 inches diagonally, which is smaller than pretty much all phones.

![Screenshot 2026-04-04 at 2.57.38 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1775294881927-ihwvnw.png)

Disappointed, I went to Alibaba, on which I found much better deals for the same stuff being sold on AliExpress. The minimum order quantities for most of these displays were also just 1, so there were pretty much no downsides. I messaged three different sellers asking for datasheets and quotes on samples, and I found the best features and value in a display from Shenzhen Startek Electronics, which sells an 8-inch 2.8K resolution flexible AMOLED with touch and a HDMI driver board for just $126 USD.

![Screenshot 2026-04-04 at 2.58.36 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1775294920146-nuup0s.png)

![Screenshot 2026-04-04 at 3.00.15 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1775295019856-q2rn40.png)

Another one of my requirements was the display having a HDMI driver board, as the MIPI DSI interfaces used by these displays requires dedicated (often custom) drivers to run on the application processor, as well as precise timing and accurate trace placement. I would much rather offload that work to an external MCU which just takes in HDMI from the application processor.

With the display more or less confimed, I then started searching for a system-on-module that is both powerful enough to run Android smoothly, but small and cheap enough to be in this project. The first choice  was to use a Raspberry Pi Compute Module 5, which is currently the industry standard with extensive community support and documentation, as well as a stable Android build. Plus, it has more than enough power to run Android easily. This, however, is WAY too expensive for this project, with 8GB RAM versions (The minimum I need for smooth Android) coming in at more than the cost of my display.

![Screenshot 2026-04-04 at 3.01.13 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1775295078311-vfc4x4.png)

I then turned to Rockchip's line of processors, which have a huge variation in module designs for them. The flagship RK3588, again, costs way too much as well as consuming too much power, but the mid-range RK3576 is just the right balance of performance and efficiency with amazing display capabilities, eight cores, and less heat generated. Moreover, the RK3576 has more modules made for it, including one amazing little 38x38mm module with 8GB of RAM that costs around $40 AUD less than the equivalent Raspberry Pi 5. The ICORE-3576Q38 by Firefly is just the right fit for this project, as the main compute board is guaranteed to be space-restricted.

![Screenshot 2026-04-04 at 3.01.46 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1775295111008-mmch7x.png)

Now, how do I power this whole thing? Even through the RK3576 is efficient, it isn't even in the same league as phone processors. Also, my huge OLED screen is guaranteed to consume a lot of power, as well as my speaker system (stay tuned for that!). So I found pretty much the perfect batteries to incude, which are thin and narrow (90x30x3mm) so they can fit between the main display and the user's arm.

![Screenshot 2026-04-04 at 3.06.24 pm](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1775295389193-4yoyzw.png)    

Now that the main 3 components of the AU_01 have been selected, I can start modelling the enclosure of the unit itself!
